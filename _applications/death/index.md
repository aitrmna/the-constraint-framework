## The Setup

The dominant assumption: consciousness is produced by the brain. When the brain stops, you stop. Game over.

I was asked to test this assumption rather than assume it.

---

## The Question

Not "Is there an afterlife?" — too vague.

A sharper question: **What is the probability that consciousness ends permanently at death?**

---

## The Method

Constraint-based reasoning:

1. Take a hypothesis ("Death = the end")
2. Identify what it **must predict** if true
3. Find domains where those predictions can be tested
4. See if the hypothesis survives

If a hypothesis predicts X, and I find not-X across multiple independent domains, the hypothesis becomes less likely.

I gave the materialist view every advantage. High starting probability. Skeptical interpretation of anomalies.

---

## What "Death = The End" Must Predict

If consciousness is destroyed at death, then:

- No verified memories should transfer between lives
- No accurate perception should occur during cardiac arrest
- Dying brains should produce only chaos, not clarity
- No information should be gained from the deceased

These are logical requirements of the hypothesis.

---

## The Evidence Domains

### 1. Verified Reincarnation Cases

Dr. Ian Stevenson and Dr. Jim Tucker at the University of Virginia documented over 2,500 cases of children reporting past-life memories. Strong cases involve children describing verifiable details of deceased persons — confirmed before any contact with the previous family. Some have birthmarks corresponding to wounds on the deceased.

**P(annihilation survives this) ≈ 15%**

### 2. Near-Death Experiences with Verified Perception

The AWARE study and others document people who report vivid experiences during cardiac arrest — including accurate descriptions of events they couldn't have witnessed. Flat EEG. No measurable brain activity. Yet perception.

**P(annihilation survives this) ≈ 10%**

### 3. Terminal Lucidity

Patients with severe dementia becoming completely lucid shortly before death — full memory, clear communication — despite physical brain damage still present. Documented in medical literature.

**P(annihilation survives this) ≈ 10%**

### 4. Deathbed Visions of Unknown Deceased

Dr. Erlendur Haraldsson documented dying patients who reported seeing recently deceased relatives — people whose deaths the patients hadn't been told about.

**P(annihilation survives this) ≈ 10%**

### 5. Mediumship Research

Triple-blind mediumship studies showing statistically significant accuracy. Sitters identify their own readings at rates far beyond chance.

**P(annihilation survives this) ≈ 15%**

### 6. Shared Death Experiences

Healthy people present at a death sharing the dying person's experience — tunnel, light, deceased relatives — despite their own brains not dying.

**P(annihilation survives this) ≈ 10%**

---

## The Calculation

Grouped by potential shared confounders, combined appropriately:

**P(annihilation) ≈ 10⁻² to 10⁻⁵**

Less than 1%. Often much less.

---

## What This Means

The probability that death is permanent annihilation is very low. Multiple independent lines of evidence would all need to be wrong — fraud, error, or coincidence — simultaneously.

---

## What This Doesn't Mean

This analysis does **not** tell us:

- What continues after death
- Whether "you" remain "you"
- Whether any religion is correct
- What the experience is like

It only reports: **Permanent annihilation appears unlikely.**

---

## A Note on the Numbers

The range **10⁻² to 10⁻⁵** represents different assumptions about independence and confounder structure.

The honest claim isn't a precise number. It's: **Given this evidence, annihilation appears very unlikely.**
